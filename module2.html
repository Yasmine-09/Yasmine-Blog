Entering Manresa with St. Ignatius Reflection

I have experienced my own version of Manresa during moments of solitude in college, especially when I felt overwhelmed, lost, and uncertain about my direction. My desolations came in the form of self-doubt, academic pressure, and the pain of losing people I once trusted, which made me question my worth and purpose. Yet, in those same quiet spaces, through prayer, reflection, and stillness, I encountered consolations that brought me peace, clarity, and a renewed sense of being guided by God. I learned to discern these inner movements by recognizing that desolation led me inward in confusion, while consolation gently drew me closer to hope, purpose, and trust in God's plan. In choosing what leads me closer to Him, I continue to grow into a life not only driven by achievement, but anchored in faith and meaning.

Sitting with Ignatius at the River Cardoner Reflection

My takeaway from the readings is that God's love is not something I earn, it is something that has always been there, even before I recognized it. Hearing "I loved you first" makes me feel both comforted and humbled, knowing that even in my lowest and most undeserving moments, I was already chosen. It softens the pressure I place on myself and reminds me that I do not have to prove my worth to be loved. At the same time, it challenges me to respond with trust rather than fear, and with openness instead of resistance. I want to respond by choosing to believe in that love daily, even when I struggle to fully understand it.

Preparing your Hearts and Minds Reflection

My takeaway from the readings is that setbacks are not meant to break me, but to redirect me toward a deeper purpose grounded in love and service. Ignatian spirituality challenges me to love first, not based on who deserves it, but because I have already been loved by God. Hearing "I loved you first" makes me feel seen and held, especially in moments when I feel inadequate or lost. It reminds me that my worth is not something I have to earn, and that I am called to extend that same love through justice, compassion, and intentional action. I want to respond by choosing a life of Magis, not just striving for more, but choosing what leads me closer to God and allows me to serve others with genuine love.

Encountering Friendship in Mission Reflection

Reading about Peter Favre and the early Jesuits made me realize that friendship is not just about closeness, but about shared purpose and growth. In my own life, the friends who have stayed, or even those I have lost, have shaped how I understand support, trust, and accountability. I see friendship as a form of mission when it encourages me to become better, to stay grounded, and to keep going even when things are difficult. It becomes leadership when I choose to be present, to listen, and to care intentionally, not just for myself but for others. In this way, friendship is no longer passive, it becomes an active way of living out purpose with others.

Finding God in Action reflection

I see God moving in my daily life through the quiet moments when I choose to pause, reflect, and respond with intention rather than impulse. As a student, He is present in my perseverance during long nights of studying and in the discipline I try to uphold despite exhaustion. As a friend, I recognize Him in the way I listen, show up, and care for others, especially in moments that require patience and understanding. In my clinical experiences, I see God most clearly in service, through compassion, presence, and the simple act of being there for someone in need. These everyday actions remind me that God is not distant, but actively present in the ordinary, calling me to live with purpose and love.

From Cave to River: 6-Day Reflection Journey

March 6: The Armor
My armor is the facade I wear every day to make people see that I'm capable of doing what they expect from me. Growing up with the belief that I always had to thrive and exceed expectations, I learned to perform strength even when I didn't feel it. People know what I'm capable of, what I know, what I can do, but rarely the things I can't. Behind that facade, I am often struggling, scared, confused, and pressured by the fear of not living up to who they think I am. What if this time, I fail and they finally see the parts of me I try to hide? In many moments, I realize I am performing instead of simply being, choosing to protect an image rather than show my reality. Today, I ask for the courage to slowly let go of that armor and trust that I am still worthy, even without it.

March 7: The Desert
Recently, life has felt both emotionally and physically depleting, leaving me in a state of interior dryness and restlessness. During our community internship visit to a rehabilitation center, I encountered individuals actively striving toward healing, each carrying their own quiet burdens. In witnessing them, I recognized that this sense of dryness is not unique to me, but a shared human experience marked by struggle, effort, and the desire for renewal. It led me to question whether God was absent in such spaces of fragility, or perhaps more subtly present within them.

In deeper reflection, I realized that God's presence is not always revealed through consolation, but often through endurance, transformation, and the courage to begin again. He is present in the discipline of recovery, in the humility of seeking help, and in the persistence to move forward despite uncertainty. Just as they are in the process of becoming, so am I, imperfect, searching, yet still guided. In this shared experience of desolation, I come to understand that God is not absent, but quietly at work, even in the most unrecognizable moments.

March 8: Cura Personalis
I often struggle with accepting my limits because I am used to performing beyond expectations, as if I am not allowed to slow down or fall short. Yet, I am reminded that I am a creature, not the Creator, finite, human, and meant to rest as much as I strive. My imperfections hold a quiet kind of beauty because they make space for growth, humility, and deeper reliance on God rather than on my own control. I see now that my limits are not barriers, but boundaries that protect me from burnout, from losing myself in constant pressure, and from believing I must carry everything alone. They teach me to pause, to ask for help, and to recognize that I am enough even in incompleteness. In honoring these limits, I begin to care for myself more fully, not just as a student who performs, but as a person who is still becoming.

March 9: The River
When I try to see my life in God's light, the things I once viewed as burdens begin to shift in meaning. My struggles in academics, relationships, and personal expectations no longer appear as mere obstacles, but as spaces where I am being formed and invited to grow. Even my past hurts and moments of rejection start to reveal lessons in resilience, humility, and deeper self-awareness. What once felt like failures now look like redirections, gently leading me toward something more aligned with who I am meant to become. I begin to see that nothing is wasted, every experience carries purpose when seen through God's presence. In this light, my life is not just a series of pressures to overcome, but a continuous unfolding of grace.

March 10: The One Greater Choice
I said yes to living, and no to dying. Heavy, wasn't it? It carries more weight than studying my mockboards. In that moment of reflection, I was brought back to the awareness that I have been clean for years, and that alone is a quiet testament to survival and grace. I realized that this is because I still have purpose, to live a life I have always dreamed of and to leave a legacy that my name can be proud of. What once felt like pressure revealed itself instead as freedom: a freedom to choose life, to continue, and to remain grounded in hope. I understand this not as coincidence, but as something aligned with God's will for me to keep moving forward in life with meaning.

March 11: Finding God in All Things
The oxygen I breathe, the wind that touches my skin, the full range of senses I often take for granted, the food I am able to eat, the school I enter each day, and the life I continue to live, all of these are quiet manifestations of God's presence and generosity. God has made all of it possible, yet I often fail to notice these graces because they have become so ordinary, so integrated into daily routine that they feel expected rather than gifted. I realize now that what I once considered "basic" is itself a continuous expression of divine provision.

Over these six days, my way of seeing has shifted from overlooking to noticing, from assuming to appreciating. I have become more aware that God is not only present in extraordinary moments, but also in the subtle, consistent details that sustain my existence. This journey has taught me that gratitude is not only a response, but a practice of perception. I now understand that to "find God in all things" is to recognize that nothing in my life is accidental, and everything carries the quiet imprint of grace.

Conversion Story

Moments That Changed You

My internship became the most transformative part of my Ateneo journey. One moment I was confidently surviving classes, and the next, I was in clinical exposure working with children with autism, suddenly learning that patience is not just a virtue, it is a survival skill. In the educational setting, I went from "I got this" to quietly panicking while administering reading tests to kindergarten students and psychological tests to applicants, hoping my training would kick in at the right time. Then came community immersion, where I was placed in unfamiliar environments that basically told me, "Welcome, adapt or cry", so I did a bit of both.

Looking back, these were not just requirements I had to pass; they were moments that humbled me, stretched me, and made me realize I do not actually know everything (shocking, I know). But somehow, in all the awkward, challenging, and slightly chaotic moments, I also discovered that I am more capable than I give myself credit for.

The Interior Struggle and Insight

As I approach graduation, my struggle has shifted toward the uncertainty of what comes next. I am faced with overwhelming options, whether to pursue medical school, work in Japan, or stay and gain experience while managing our family business, and each path carries its own weight and expectations. In this process, I found myself doubting my capabilities, questioning if I am truly ready or deserving of any of these opportunities. There were moments of desolation marked by confusion, fear of choosing wrong, and the pressure to succeed. Yet, I also experienced consolations in quiet clarity, that my worth is not defined by a single decision, and that growth comes from choosing with intention rather than fear. This struggle is teaching me to trust not only my abilities, but also the process of discernment, believing that where I am led is where I am meant to grow.

A New Way of Seeing

When I first entered Ateneo, I was basically a dependent, slightly spoiled "Disney princess", everything was convenient, everything was provided, and I truly believed life would just work itself out for me. Fast forward to now, I somehow evolved into someone who can survive on her own, make decisions, and function without a personal support system on standby (character development, finally). Along the way, I realized that life is not just about being taken care of, it is about learning how to stand on your own feet, even when it is uncomfortable or unfamiliar.

This shift changed how I see myself: not just as someone who performs well or is expected to succeed, but as someone who can adapt, grow, and handle real-life responsibilities. I have learned that becoming is messy, sometimes humbling, and often unexpected, but it is also where real strength is formed. From a "Disney princess" waiting for things to be done for me, I have become someone who shows up, figures things out, and keeps going anyway.
